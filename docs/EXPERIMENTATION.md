## Purpose
This document defines the experimentation philosophy of Vector Labs Studio. It answers a single question: **How do we learn responsibly when certainty is impossible?** It establishes the principles we use to structure experiments as one disciplined way to create relevant evidence when important uncertainty remains. Rather than detailing testing platforms, statistical methods, or engineering implementation details, these guidelines outline how we approach experimentation to support decision-making.

---

## 1. Learning and Confirmation
* **Learning over Confirmation**: The primary purpose of experimentation is to generate learning, not to confirm pre-existing beliefs or justify past choices. We value learning that updates our understanding over evidence that merely validates our current path.
* **Decision Relevance**: Experiments should examine uncertainties and assumptions that materially affect the decision they are intended to inform. We do not require a formal hypothesis structure or specific experimental format, but we ensure experimentation is not shaped merely to protect a preferred conclusion or preserve an existing position.
* **Informing Choices**: Experimental outcomes do not directly determine future choices. Instead, they inform judgment when interpreted alongside evidence quality, context, constraints, competing evidence, and the significance of the decision.

---

## 2. Proportionality and Risk Management
* **Proportionate Design**: The scale and rigor of an experiment should be proportionate to uncertainty, risk, reversibility, cost, scope, and decision significance. We avoid disproportionate investigation or unnecessary rigor for low-impact, easily reversible choices, focusing instead on sufficient and responsible learning.
* **Preserving Options**: Where practical, experiments should preserve meaningful options and limit exposure to consequences that cannot be easily reversed, recognizing that not every consequence is fully reversible.
* **Value of Negative Outcomes**: Negative or unexpected outcomes can create value when they yield reliable, relevant, and decision-useful evidence that is proportionate to the cost and exposure of the experiment. Such outcomes help prevent disproportionate investment in directions that are not sufficiently supported by current evidence or do not justify further commitment at the present time.

---

## 3. Decisions and Judgment
* **Evolving Understanding**: We recognize that single experiments rarely provide complete answers. Understanding develops through successive and varied investigations, where new evidence may reinforce, refine, contextualize, or challenge earlier conclusions.
* **Decision Support**: Experimentation is a tool to support decisions under uncertainty; it does not replace the responsibility of making them. Experimental results do not automatically dictate path selection, but serve as inputs to the human judgment required to evaluate options.
* **Complementing Judgment**: Experimentation complements, rather than replaces, experience and reasoned judgment. We combine experimental evidence with context, constraints, experience, and reasoned judgment to support responsible decisions under uncertainty.

---

## Scope Boundaries
This document intentionally does not define:

- Specific user exposure, routing, or deployment methods
- Analytics architectures, metric definitions, or tracking instrumentation
- Statistical formulas, mathematical models, or significance testing criteria
- Software tools, experimentation platforms, or vendor selections
- Step-by-step engineering or operational procedures for running tests

Where needed, implementation-specific guidance may be defined separately without changing these principles.
