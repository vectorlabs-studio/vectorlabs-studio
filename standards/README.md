# Vector Labs Studio Standards

This document defines the foundation for all standards within Vector Labs Studio. It answers a single question: **What is a standard, and how should standards be created and maintained within the Studio?** It establishes the distinction between handbook principles and common practice, ensuring a consistent framework for our shared guidance.

---

## Principles and Practices

To guide activity effectively, we separate our guidance into two distinct, cooperative layers:

*   **Principles (The Handbook)**: The handbook defines why the Studio exists, how we think, and how decisions are made. Principles guide intent and judgment, providing the philosophical and strategic foundations of the Studio.
*   **Standards (Common Practice)**: Standards translate that guidance into common approaches for recurring areas of work. They represent practical, domain-specific patterns that implement handbook principles in daily practice.

---

## The Nature of a Standard

A standard is a shared approach to a recurring concern, informed by accumulated experience, available evidence, recurring needs, explicit trade-offs, and deliberate judgment. We design our standards to be:

*   **Practical and Opinionated**: Standards select specific paths to reduce cognitive overhead, manage complexity, and support consistency across related work.
*   **Durable but Adaptive**: Standards act as working baselines that encourage consistency while leaving room for thoughtful, reasoned exceptions. They should remain open to improvement as stronger evidence and experience become available.
*   **Focused**: A standard has a single, clear responsibility. It avoids mixing unrelated domains. When guidance within a standard grows to cover distinct concerns, it should be split into separate, modular standards rather than extending indefinitely.

---

## Relationships Between Standards

To maintain a cohesive and clear knowledge base, standards should operate as a cooperative network:

*   **General to Specific**: General standards may provide foundations for more specific standards. Specific standards should not duplicate broader guidance.
*   **Referential Integrity**: Cross-references should be preferred over repeated content to prevent duplication and drift.
*   **Explicit Boundaries**: The scope and boundaries of each standard should remain explicit.
*   **Handbook Consistency**: All standards must remain consistent with the handbook. Conflict between a standard and the handbook should trigger clarification or revision rather than silent contradiction.

---

## When to Introduce or Decline a Standard

Standards should be introduced selectively to ensure they provide clear, durable value.

### When to Introduce a Standard
A new standard may be valuable when a recurring area of work would benefit from:
*   reduced repeated decision-making;
*   clearer quality expectations;
*   shared trade-offs;
*   more consistent outputs;
*   clearer boundaries between related concerns.

Guidance should be established only when there is sufficient experience, recurrence, or durable value to justify becoming shared guidance. We avoid standardizing a practice solely because it is new or emerging.

### When to Decline a Standard
A new standard should not be created when:
*   the concern is temporary;
*   the guidance is project-specific;
*   the topic is already covered elsewhere;
*   the pattern represents primarily personal preference;
*   there is insufficient experience to establish durable guidance.

---

## What Belongs in a Standard

Standards align practice without prescribing rigid step-by-step procedures.

### What is Included
*   **Preferred Patterns**: The established, shared approaches to solving common problems in a given domain.
*   **Context and Trade-offs**: The reasoning behind chosen patterns, including why alternative approaches were deselected.
*   **Scope and Boundaries**: Clear definitions of where the standard applies and where its boundaries end.

### What is Excluded
*   **Principles**: Standards do not redefine core values, decision frameworks, or philosophies. They must reference, and remain consistent with, the handbook.
*   **Temporary Workflows**: Short-term instructions, tool configurations, or transient project-specific rules belong in checklists or templates, not in durable standards.
*   **Personal Preference**: Practices are standardized only when they resolve real-world complexity, build leverage, or improve quality, not to enforce arbitrary aesthetic preferences.

---

## Exceptions and Deviations

A standard establishes a common path, not a constraint on judgment.

Thoughtful exceptions are allowed when context warrants them. Significant or reusable deviations should capture their reasoning when doing so improves future decisions, quality, or shared understanding. We avoid creating process overhead for small, local choices.
