## Purpose
This document defines the discovery philosophy of Vector Labs Studio. It answers a single question: **How do we discover what is worth pursuing?** It establishes the principles we use to reduce uncertainty, surface and examine assumptions, clarify opportunities, and improve the basis for decisions before making meaningful commitments. Rather than detailing research techniques or discovery methods, these principles guide how we build confidence in our product direction without attempting to prove assumptions or confirm product viability prematurely.

---

## 1. Curiosity and Uncertainty Reduction
* **Reducing Uncertainty**: Product discovery is the active process of reducing uncertainty about user needs, opportunities, and constraints. Proposed solutions are examined to learn from them, but they must remain open to challenge, revision, or rejection as our understanding improves, preventing discovery from merely justifying preferred solutions.
* **Curiosity Before Confidence**: We approach new opportunities with open-minded curiosity. We accept that our initial understanding is incomplete and that deliberate learning builds sufficient confidence for the next decision while accepting that uncertainty cannot be fully eliminated.
* **Explicit Assumptions**: We make our beliefs, assumptions, and hypotheses explicit so the Studio can identify which uncertainties materially affect the next decision, its risk, its reversibility, and the size of the commitment. Some uncertainty may remain when it is proportionate to the decision.

---

## 2. Evidence and Investigation
* **Evidence-Driven Direction**: We update our understanding when relevant evidence contradicts prior beliefs, adapting our hypotheses or pivoting our focus accordingly.
* **Co-evolving Opportunities and Responses**: We seek to understand opportunities deeply while resisting premature solution commitments. Rather than demanding a strict sequence where a problem is fully understood before any solution thinking occurs, we allow our understanding of the opportunity and possible responses to evolve together, preventing early attachment to a preferred answer.
* **Proportionate Investigation**: The scale and rigor of our learning effort should be proportionate to uncertainty, risk, scope, reversibility, and the significance of the next decision. The objective is sufficient learning to make the next choice, rather than minimum effort.

---

## 3. Decisions and Boundaries
* **Acceptable Uncertainty**: Discovery is not an endless search for absolute certainty. We stop discovery when the remaining uncertainty is acceptable relative to the risk, scope, and reversibility of the next decision.
* **Value of Negative Evidence**: Reliable negative evidence is valuable when it improves understanding and prevents disproportionate investment in opportunities unlikely to create sufficient value. We avoid relying on incomplete or low-quality investigation as evidence.
* **Supporting Decisions**: Discovery supports decisions; it does not make them. The relevant evidence, observations, constraints, and judgment guide our choice, but commitments still require trade-offs and strategic risk evaluation.
* **Outcome Uncertainty**: We acknowledge that discovery cannot guarantee successful products. It reduces risk and improves decisions, but final outcomes remain influenced by factors discovery cannot fully predict or control.

---

## Scope Boundaries
This document intentionally does not define:

- Specific user research, interviewing, or surveying methodologies
- Qualitative or quantitative customer profiling frameworks
- Step-by-step discovery workshops or ideation processes
- Tactical prioritization templates or opportunity mapping tools
- Specific analytics tools, metrics systems, or roadmap formats

Where needed, implementation-specific guidance may be defined separately without changing these principles.
