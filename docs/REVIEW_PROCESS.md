## Purpose
This document defines the philosophy of review within Vector Labs Studio. We treat review not as a gatekeeping or administrative approval process, but as a collaborative, knowledge-building, and confidence-building activity. This document establishes the principles of how work earns confidence before becoming part of the Studio's products, operations, or shared memory.

---

## 1. The Core Philosophy: Confidence Over Authority
Review exists to build collective confidence in a piece of work. It is not an exercise of personal or hierarchical authority.
* **Building Confidence**: The goal of review is to build justified confidence, reduce uncertainty, and identify weaknesses before work becomes more difficult or costly to change. Review does not guarantee absolute correctness, but raises the collective baseline of the Studio.
* **Evidence-Based Evaluation**: Review is a process of gathering relevant quantitative and qualitative evidence and validating assumptions, rather than seeking arbitrary permission. Work is evaluated against outcomes, measurements, observations, domain knowledge, explicit assumptions, user evidence, and reasoned judgment, allowing us to consider value, risk, consistency, clarity, and strategic alignment.
* **Refining the Artifact**: Review is focused entirely on the work, not the contributor. It is a constructive mechanism to refine systems and documentation, fostering a culture of mutual improvement and shared learning.

---

## 2. Proportionality and Continuous Engagement
Review is integrated into how we work, rather than acting as a final barrier to delivery.
* **Early and Proportional Review**: Review is not a single gatekeeping event at the end of execution. Engaging early with assumptions and specifications reduces downstream waste, with the frequency and format of engagement kept proportional to the significance of the work.
* **Proportional Rigor**: The depth of review should reflect factors such as risk, uncertainty, impact, novelty, and reversibility, rather than work size alone. Routine, low-risk, and reversible work should carry minimal review overhead, while highly uncertain or irreversible choices deserve more deliberate evaluation.
* **Significant Artifacts**: Artifacts that materially affect the Studio's products, core systems, operating principles, or durable memory should receive review proportional to their significance and long-term impact.

---

## 3. Divergent Thinking and Shared Accountability
Friction and discussion during review are valuable mechanisms for refining quality and building shared understanding.
* **Constructive Disagreement**: Healthy friction and divergent opinions are valuable. Disagreement improves outcomes when reasoning is made transparent, trade-offs are explicitly analyzed, and evidence is prioritized over subjective preferences.
* **Preserving Rationale**: When a review materially changes, challenges, or validates a major decision, the relevant context, trade-offs, assumptions, and rationale should be preserved in the appropriate durable documentation. This ensures that future contributors can understand the decision context and conditions shaping the current state, without turning temporary review discussions into permanent documentation.
* **Accountability and Contributions**: Review does not transfer ownership. The person or role responsible for the work remains accountable for its outcome, while all participants remain responsible for the quality and integrity of their own contributions. Reviewers do not become owners merely by participating in the review.

---

## Scope Boundaries
This document intentionally does not define:

- Specific code review procedures or interfaces
- Branching, merging, or repository permission structures
- Pull request formats or approval checklist templates
- Automated quality gates, linters, or CI/CD system integrations
- Specific collaboration, chat, or issue tracking tools

Where needed, implementation-specific guidance may be defined separately without changing these principles.
