## Purpose
This document defines how products evolve throughout their lifetime inside Vector Labs Studio. We view products as long-lived, continuously improved systems rather than finite projects with fixed end dates. This lifecycle establishes the stages of a product's journey—from the initial discovery of an opportunity to its eventual retirement—emphasizing learning, value creation, and contribution back to the Studio.

---

## 1. Inception: Problem-First Discovery
Every product inside the Studio originates from an identified, real-world problem or opportunity. We do not build products in search of a problem. Instead, we begin by articulating and documenting the user friction, the hypothesized value, and the boundary constraints. This discovery phase is lightweight and focused on establishing a clear foundation of assumptions that can be tested with minimal initial effort.

---

## 2. Evolution: Continuous Learning and Adaptation
Products are living systems that evolve continuously as we gather real-world usage data and feedback.
* **Continuous Evolution**: Once launched, a product does not enter a static maintenance mode; it is subject to ongoing refinement based on feedback and operational reality.
* **Releases as Milestones**: A release is not a finish line or the completion of a project. It is simply a milestone that validates our assumptions and exposes the system to user reality. Each release is a tool for learning and a generator of new feedback.
* **Adapting to Evidence**: If evidence shows that user needs have shifted, or that our original assumptions were incorrect, the product must adapt. We do not commit to a static roadmap; we adjust the product's direction dynamically based on logical evaluation and observed outcome quality.

---

## 3. Compounding Value: Reusable Assets and shared knowledge
A product's value is not measured solely by its external utility to users. Its lifecycle must also enrich the Studio's internal capabilities.
* **Generating Reusable Assets**: As products mature, they generate components, automation scripts, and design patterns. These are generalized and moved to the Studio's shared infrastructure to accelerate future products.
* **Knowledge Contribution**: Every success, workflow bottleneck, and system failure encountered during a product's life must be translated into shared knowledge. Lessons learned are documented and integrated back into the Studio's operating model, raising the baseline capability of all future builds.

---

## 4. Retirement: The Healthy End of Life
We view product retirement as a natural, healthy stage of the product lifecycle rather than a failure.
* **Criteria for Retirement**: A product is retired when it no longer addresses a meaningful user need, when its maintenance cost exceeds its value, or when it has been superseded by a simpler and more effective system.
* **Responsible Wind-Down**: Retirement involves transitioning users responsibly, archiving data, and decommissioning infrastructure cleanly.
* **Retrospective Capture**: The final act of a product's lifecycle is a comprehensive capture of its history, design patterns, and operational lessons, ensuring its knowledge remains a permanent part of the Studio.

---

## 5. Ultimate Success
A successful product achieves two key outcomes:
* **User Value**: It delivers simple, reliable, and high-value solutions that resolve user friction over the long term.
* **Studio Strengthening**: It strengthens the Studio by leaving behind a legacy of reusable assets, improved workflows, and validated shared knowledge, making the company more capable of building the next generation of software.

---

## Scope Boundaries
This document intentionally does not define:

- Software development lifecycles (SDLC)

- Roadmap or backlog management processes
- CI/CD, testing, or deployment pipeline structures
- Specific release engineering guidelines
- Team topologies or individual roles

These topics are documented separately and build upon this high-level product lifecycle.
