# Design Standard

This standard defines the shared practices for designing product experiences within Vector Labs Studio. It answers a single question: **How should product experiences be designed within Vector Labs Studio?** It builds upon the principles of the handbook and follows the Standards Foundation, translating product intent into understandable, usable, coherent, and inclusive experiences.

---

## The Purpose of Design

We treat design not as cosmetic decoration or the production of static visual artifacts, but as the deliberate shaping of how people understand, navigate, interact with, and experience a product. Design work should be:

*   **Purpose-Driven**: Aligning the experience directly with intended product outcomes and stakeholder needs.
*   **Human-Centered**: Designing for the capabilities, constraints, and contexts of the actual people who use the product.
*   **Evidence-Informed and Iterative**: Using observations and feedback to refine experiences progressively as understanding grows.
*   **Proportional**: Matching the depth of design exploration and validation effort to the decision's impact, uncertainty, and cost of being wrong.

---

## Starting from Intent and Context

Design decisions should begin by clarifying the context of the experience:

*   **Stakeholder Context**: Identify who is affected and understand their needs, expectations, and operational constraints.
*   **Action Support**: Define the specific decisions, tasks, or choices the experience must support.
*   **Existing Patterns**: Review current product behaviors and conventions to maintain coherence.
*   **Risk and Reversibility**: Assess the consequences of user actions, matching design rigor to the reversibility of the outcome.

---

## Clarity and System State

Experiences should make their purpose, current state, and available actions immediately understandable to the user:

*   **Information Hierarchy**: Structure content to highlight the most relevant information, using progressive disclosure to manage density when essential complexity remains.
*   **Understandable Language**: Use clear, concise terminology that matches the user's context, avoiding internal jargon.
*   **Actionability and Predictability**: Ensure interactive elements are recognizable and their consequences are predictable.
*   **Continuous Feedback**: Communicate what is happening and what has happened. The experience should make it clear whether an action was received, if work is in progress, whether it succeeded or failed, and what can be done next. Feedback prominence should reflect the significance and uncertainty of the action.

---

## Usability and Task Success

Design should support people in completing tasks with confidence, appropriate effort, and ease:

*   **Learnability and Efficiency**: Design paths that are discoverable for new users while remaining efficient for experienced users.
*   **Uninterrupted and Interrupted Flows**: Account for realistic usage conditions, including environmental constraints, distraction, and the need to pause and continue work.
*   **Error Prevention and Recovery**: Prefer preventing errors through clear defaults and guidance. When mistakes occur, support clear problem recognition, understandable consequences, and safe, proportionate recovery pathways. Use confirmations only when the action's consequence and risk justify interruption.

---

## Coherence and Purposeful Consistency

Related components of a product should communicate and behave as a single, coherent system:

*   **Pattern Reuse**: Reuse established design patterns and conventions before creating new ones to help users transfer prior understanding.
*   **Purposeful Variation**: Consistency should not force identical experiences in differing contexts. Deliberate variation is appropriate when the user need, meaning, risk, or platform environment materially differs.
*   **Managing Variation**: New design patterns should be introduced only when existing patterns cannot satisfy the requirements, avoiding unnecessary conceptual fragmentation.

---

## Foundational Accessibility and Inclusion

Accessibility is a fundamental quality of design that must be integrated from the beginning, rather than treated as a later adjustment:

*   **Inclusive Design Thinking**: Design decisions should account for variations in physical, sensory, and cognitive abilities, as well as temporary or situational limitations.
*   **Diverse Contexts**: Avoid assuming a single ideal user, device, or mode of interaction. Support diverse environments, devices, and cognitive loads without prescribing platform-specific implementation details in this standard.

---

## Managing Complexity

Design should reduce unnecessary complexity rather than merely hiding it behind a minimal visual interface:

*   **Visual Minimalism vs. Conceptual Simplicity**: A visually clean interface can still be conceptually complex or difficult to use. Focus on reducing the number of concepts a user must hold in memory.
*   **Orientation and Context**: Keep users oriented within the system structure, ensuring they understand where they are, how they arrived, and how to return.
*   **Exposing System Structure**: Avoid exposing internal technical architecture unless understanding it directly helps the user complete their task.

---

## Evidence and Design Trade-offs

Important design choices should be informed by evidence and clear trade-off reasoning:

*   **Gathering Evidence**: Evaluate important design assumptions using methods proportionate to uncertainty and impact, such as user feedback, direct observation, usability evaluation, existing conventions, or professional judgment.
*   **Explicit Trade-offs**: Acknowledge that design involves balancing competing qualities—such as simplicity and capability, consistency and contextual appropriateness, or speed and safety. Product experiences should be explainable by the trade-offs accepted.

---

## Evolution of Experiences

Design should evolve as products, users, evidence, and constraints change:

*   **Intentional Change**: Evaluate whether existing patterns remain effective. Introduce design updates only when the benefits of the change outweigh the learning cost and friction imposed on users.
*   **Compatibility and Transition**: Ensure transitions or migrations between old and new experiences are managed smoothly, preserving consistency across related flows.

---

## Evaluating Design Quality

Design quality is evaluated by the user experience it creates, rather than the visual polish of its static assets. Design reviews should evaluate:

*   **Alignment**: How well the experience supports the intended product outcomes.
*   **Clarity and State**: Whether hierarchy, available actions, and system states are clear.
*   **Usability and Accessibility**: How effectively the design supports tasks, error recovery, and diverse user needs.
*   **Coherence and Trade-offs**: Whether the design maintains referential integrity with established patterns and presents clear, reasoned trade-offs.
