## Purpose
This document defines the foundation for all standards within Vector Labs Studio. It answers a single question: **What is a standard, and how should standards be created and maintained within the Studio?** It establishes the distinction between handbook principles and operational practice, ensuring a consistent framework for our technical and operational standards.

---

## 1. Principles and Practices

To guide activity effectively, we separate our guidance into two distinct, cooperative layers:

*   **Principles (The Handbook)**: The handbook defines *why* the Studio exists, *how* we think, and how decisions are made. Principles are timeless, stable, and conceptual, establishing the boundaries of acceptable action.
*   **Standards (Common Practice)**: Standards define *how* work is commonly performed. They represent opinionated, practical patterns that implement handbook principles in specific operational contexts. While principles govern intent, standards govern expression.

---

## 2. The Nature of a Standard

A standard is a shared, validated approach to a recurring problem. We design our standards to be:

*   **Practical and Opinionated**: Standards are not neutral summaries; they select specific paths to reduce cognitive overhead, manage complexity, and ensure interoperability.
*   **Durable but Adaptive**: Standards are based on accumulated operational experience rather than personal preference. They are not immutable rules, but working baselines that encourage consistency while leaving room for thoughtful, reasoned exceptions.
*   **Focused**: A standard has a single, clear responsibility. It avoids mixing unrelated domains. When guidance within a standard grows to cover distinct concerns, it should be split into separate, modular standards rather than extending indefinitely.

---

## 3. What Belongs in a Standard

Standards exist to align execution without prescribing rigid step-by-step procedures.

### What is Included
*   **Preferred Patterns**: The established, validated approaches to solving common problems in a given domain.
*   **Context and Trade-offs**: The reasoning behind chosen patterns, including why alternative approaches were deselected.
*   **Scope and Boundaries**: Clear definitions of where the standard applies and where its boundaries end.

### What is Excluded
*   **Principles**: Standards do not redefine core values, decision frameworks, or philosophies. They must reference, and remain consistent with, the handbook.
*   **Temporary Workflows**: Short-term instructions, tool configurations, or transient project-specific rules belong in operational checklists, not in durable standards.
*   **Personal Preference**: Patterns are standardized only when they resolve real-world complexity, build leverage, or improve quality, not to enforce arbitrary aesthetic preferences.

---

## 4. Evolution of Standards

Standards are living documents that evolve alongside our operational capability.

*   **Introducing a Standard**: A new standard is introduced when a recurring problem generates unnecessary decision friction, or when emerging practices require alignment to preserve built-in quality.
*   **Continuous Improvement**: Standards are refined, simplified, or retired when new evidence, technologies, or operational lessons show that the current approach is no longer effective.
*   **Thoughtful Exceptions**: A standard establishes a common path, not a constraint on judgment. Contributors may deviate from a standard when the unique context of a task warrants an alternative solution, provided the decision is reasoned, documented, and aligned with handbook principles.
