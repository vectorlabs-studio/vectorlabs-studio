## Purpose
This document defines the discovery philosophy of Vector Labs Studio. It answers a single question: **How do we discover what is worth pursuing?** It establishes the principles we use to reduce uncertainty, validate assumptions, and clarify opportunities before making meaningful product commitments. Rather than detailing research techniques or discovery methods, these principles guide how we build confidence in our product direction.

---

## 1. Curiosity and Uncertainty Reduction
* **Reducing Uncertainty**: Product discovery is the active process of reducing uncertainty about user needs, opportunities, and constraints. We do not engage in discovery to validate pre-existing solutions, but to gather evidence.
* **Curiosity Before Certainty**: We approach new opportunities with open-minded curiosity. We accept that our initial understanding is incomplete and that establishing certainty requires deliberate learning and investigation.
* **Explicit Assumptions**: We make our beliefs, assumptions, and hypotheses explicit. By listing what we assume to be true, we expose the gaps in our knowledge and identify what must be verified before making commitments.

---

## 2. Evidence and Investigation
* **Evidence-Driven Direction**: We allow evidence to change our direction. When findings contradict our assumptions, we adapt our hypotheses or pivot our focus. We value truth over consistency.
* **Understanding Problems First**: We seek to understand the problem deeply before committing to a solution. A premature focus on build structures or solutions closes down learning and creates unnecessary complexity.
* **Proportional Investigation**: We prefer small, targeted investigations over large, expensive commitments. We invest minimal resources to answer core questions, ensuring our research scale matches the uncertainty of the opportunity.

---

## 3. Decisions and Boundaries
* **Acceptable Uncertainty**: Discovery is not an endless search for absolute certainty. We stop discovery when the remaining uncertainty is acceptable relative to the risk, scope, and reversibility of the next decision.
* **Value of Negative Evidence**: Disproving an opportunity or showing a problem is not worth solving is a successful discovery outcome. Negative evidence creates high value by saving resources and preventing the construction of useless systems.
* **Informing Decisions**: Discovery informs decisions; it does not make them. The evidence collected guides our judgment, but final commitments still require trade-offs, risk evaluation, and strategic choices.
* **No Guarantees**: We acknowledge that discovery cannot guarantee product success. It reduces the risk of building the wrong thing, but market realities and execution quality ultimately decide the outcome.

---

## Scope Boundaries
This document intentionally does not define:

- Customer interviews, surveys, or usability testing techniques
- Frameworks like Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) or Persona development
- Discovery workshop agendas, design thinking, or lean startup processes
- Opportunity Solution Trees or prioritization templates
- Analytics tools, product metrics, or roadmap planning models

Where needed, implementation-specific guidance may be defined separately without changing these principles.
